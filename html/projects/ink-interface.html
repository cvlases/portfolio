Ink & Interface

What if tattooing and body art traditions held a richer, more human starting point for XR gestures than the gesture vocabularies we use now?

TeamMHCI capstone + Arm

// understand

This project began with a suspicion: HCI keeps inventing “natural” XR gestures while overlooking entire traditions of deliberate, socially legible movement that already exist on the body.

I grounded that question in fieldwork at tattoo conventions and conversations with tattoo artists, collectors, and XR designers. Instead of treating the body as a blank interface surface, the project treats it as cultural, expressive, and already encoded with meaning.

// define

Framing the problem

The goal was not “add tattoos to XR.” It was to ask how body-art practices might reshape the way we think about gesture design, social acceptability, and bystander legibility.

Outputs

The research synthesizes into 18 design principles across four categories, giving the project a real design language rather than a loose inspiration moodboard.

The core development move was a Wizard-of-Oz ecological study exploring on-skin gesture social acceptability, including a novel bystander-legibility condition. That gives the project both conceptual depth and methodological teeth.
